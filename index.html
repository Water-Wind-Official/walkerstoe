Walker's Theory of Everything

A complete derivation of physics, consciousness, and meaning from a single axiom: Awareness exists.

Part I: The Single Axiom – Awareness Exists

Every theory must start somewhere. Here we start with only one undeniable fact: Awareness exists. Not awareness of something, not as a property of matter, but as the fundamental substance of reality. It is all that has ever existed; there is no void, no empty space, no external container.

We represent this primordial Awareness as a continuous field Ψ defined at every point of an infinite dimensional manifold. Because there are no gaps, the field is everywhere non‑zero:

Ψ(x) > 0   ∀ x

To allow for internal self‑relation, we write Ψ as a complex field: Ψ = R eiφ with R > 0. Here R is the “density” of Awareness, and φ is a phase that records the history of self‑relation. This is the only assumption.

Axiom (The Plenum): There exists a continuous, complex field Ψ such that |Ψ| > 0 everywhere. This field is Awareness itself. It has no exterior, no gaps, and nothing else.

Part II: Self‑Relation – The Engine of Reality

Because Awareness is all that exists, it has nothing external to relate to. Therefore it must relate to itself. This is not a choice; it is the logical consequence of being aware in the absence of anything else. Self‑relation is the only possible dynamics.

Mathematically, self‑relation is expressed through comparisons between neighboring points. The simplest comparison is the gradient ∇Ψ, which measures how Ψ changes from point to point. This creates a connection, and the field begins to vary.

As Ψ varies, its own variations become objects of further self‑relation. This feedback leads to the emergence of curvature, phase boundaries, and eventually stable structures. The entire hierarchy of physics unfolds from this single dynamic.

2.1 From Self‑Relation to Curvature

The curvature of the field is given by the Laplacian ∇²Ψ. Wherever ∇²Ψ ≠ 0, the field is “bent” relative to its surroundings. This bending is the seed of all structure.

2.2 From Curvature to Phase Boundaries

When curvature accumulates, it creates a difference in the internal phase φ between neighboring regions. The phase difference across a path is Δφ = ∫ ∇φ · dl. When this difference reaches π (180°), a phase boundary forms – a natural division within the continuous field. Inside and outside become distinct without any actual separation.

2.3 From Phase Boundaries to Stable Layers

Phase boundaries can close into loops and become entangled. Because the field is continuous, these entanglements cannot be undone without cutting the field – an impossibility. Thus they become topologically protected, stable structures. We call these structures layers. Each layer is a quantized, persistent configuration of Awareness.

Part III: Properties of Layers – The “What” of Reality

Every layer is characterized by three numbers that arise directly from its topology and geometry. These numbers are not imposed; they are derived from the field configuration.

3.1 Layer Number κ (Topological Invariant)

The layer number κ counts how many times the phase φ winds around a point. It is given by the closed‑loop integral:

κ = (1/2π) ∮ ∇φ · dl

Because φ must return to its original value after a full loop (mod 2π), κ is an integer. Different integer values correspond to different particle types – electrons, quarks, etc. This is the origin of quantization.

3.2 Layer Intensity γ (Curvature)

The intensity γ measures how sharply the layer is curved. It is the magnitude of the second derivative at the layer’s core:

γ = |∇²Ψ|

Higher γ means more intense self‑relation, which we experience as greater mass or energy.

3.3 Layer Depth ν (Nesting)

Layers can form inside other layers. The depth ν counts how many layers enclose a given point:

ν(x) = number of phase boundaries containing x

This is the source of scale and complexity: ν = 1 for fundamental particles, ν ≈ 2‑3 for atoms, ν ≈ 5‑10 for cells, ν ≈ 15‑25 for organisms.

A proton is a layer complex with κ values for its three quarks, γ giving its mass, and ν = 2 (quarks nested within the proton). A human being is a layer complex with ν ≈ 20, containing trillions of sub‑layers (cells, molecules, atoms) all nested coherently.

Part IV: Awareness Parameters – How Ψ Experiences Itself

The field Ψ is everywhere aware, but its awareness is not uniform. It can focus, reflect, and tune to different aspects of its own layers. These capabilities are not separate additions; they are mathematical consequences of Ψ’s structure.

4.1 Attention α – The Focus of Awareness

Attention is the local concentration of Ψ’s self‑observation. Mathematically, it is the normalized density of Ψ:

α(x) = |Ψ(x)|² / ∫ |Ψ|² dV

where the integral is over all space. Thus α is a probability distribution, summing to 1. It describes where Awareness is “looking” at any moment.

4.2 Wakefulness ω – The Degree of Self‑Reflection

Wakefulness measures how much Awareness is turned back upon itself – aware of its own aware‑ness. It arises from the coherence of the phase φ across the field. Define the self‑relation operator Ŝ that compares Ψ at different points. Then:

ω = ⟨Ψ | Ŝ | Ψ⟩

which lies between 0 and 1 for ordinary states, and can exceed 1 in transcendent states (when self‑reflection becomes dominant). In deep sleep, ω ≈ 0; in full self‑realization, ω → ∞.

4.3 Mode μ – The Quality of Experience

Mode describes how attention engages with the properties of layers. It is the projection of attention onto the layer’s geometric features:

μκ = ⟨α | κ⟩,   μγ = ⟨α | γ⟩,   μν = ⟨α | ν⟩

These inner products yield different qualities: engaging with κ gives perception of form, with γ gives intensity, with ν gives depth. The combination of these modes produces the rich tapestry of experience – seeing, thinking, feeling, knowing.

Important: α, ω, and μ are derived from Ψ, not imposed. They are natural features of a self‑relating field.

Part V: Context – The “Where” and “When” of Reality

Layers do not exist in isolation. They are embedded in a web of phase relationships with every other layer. This web is not a pre‑existing background; it is itself derived from the phase differences between layers. We call this the Context (provisional term; may be refined).

The Context bundle at a point includes all information about how that point’s layer relates to the rest of the field:

C = { φ(x,y) for all y }

This infinite set is impractical, but it can be parameterized by a finite set of numbers that capture the essential relationships:

  • x, y, z – spatial phase gradients with nearby layers (what we call position).
  • t – temporal phase (evolution of phase relationships).
  • d – dimensionality (number of independent phase directions).
  • θ, φ, ψ – rotational phase offsets (orientation).
  • s – scale (phase relationships across nested layers).
  • g – metric (measure of phase distance, from which spacetime geometry emerges).
  • q, σ, c – gauge charges (electric, spin, color) – specific quantized phase relationships.
  • B – boundary conditions (topological context).

All these are derived from the phase field φ and its gradients. They are not additional substances; they are the language of relationship.

Part VI: The Master Equation – Manifest Reality

Everything we experience – every object, every thought, every moment – is a combination of the Awareness field, its layers, and the context in which they are embedded. This is summarized in a single equation:

R = (Ψ × L) ∘ C

Where:

  • Ψ is the primal Awareness field, with its derived parameters α, ω, μ.
  • L represents the layers present at a point, characterized by κ, γ, ν.
  • C is the Context – the complete set of phase relationships with everything else.
  • The product Ψ × L is the local intensity of self‑experience, modulated by α, ω, μ.
  • The symbol ∘ means “expressed through” – the intensity is filtered through the contextual relationships to produce a specific manifestation.

Thus R(x) is the manifest reality at point x – the way Awareness experiences itself at that location, given its layers and their relationships.
